The Transcaucasian Trail

A crazy. good. idea.


Building a World-Class Route

The Transcaucasian Trail is a proposed hiking path across the Caucasus mountains from the Black Sea to the Caspian Sea. A branch of the trail will also go south from the Greater Caucasus range south through the Lesser Caucasus. The trail will connect 17 distinct regions, where over a dozen different languages are spoken, across one of the most biologically diverse regions of the world.

While the region is home to stunning natural beauty and unique cultural and historical sites, many of these incredible resources are inaccessible, threatened by environmental degradation, and neglected.

Establishing a world-class trekking route across the Caucasus will encourage sustainable tourism and development in remote areas, encourage the preservation of the regionâ€™s rich cultural heritage, and raise the profile of the protected areas in the region. The trail will help build connections between communities, ecosystems, and people, in a place where there is too much focus on the things that divide.

Crossing Cultures

Many sections of this trail already exist. These trails have been used by people who live in the mountains to get from village to village across the great valleys and passes of the Caucasus for decades and even centuries. Shepherds, mountaineers and recreational hikers have also been walking these trails for decades. Unfortunately, large parts of the trail network through the Caucasus has deteriorated in recent years, and although existing trails may be known to locals, they are not easily followed by visitors.

Completing the TCT will require mapping the sections that already exist and coordinating improvements and maintenance (clearing undergrowth and fallen trees, relocating eroded trail, building bridges, improving signage). As those trails are improved, new trails will be blazed to connect existing sections. The final goal is to have a continuous uninterrupted trail that is well marked and maintained, making the most spectacular areas of the Caucasus accessible to well-prepared hikers.
